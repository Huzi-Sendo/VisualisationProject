Overview
The Big Idea

Instead of adding invisible dark matter to explain why galaxies spin faster than they should, this theory asks: what if gravity itself gets a little stronger for very massive objects? The Enhanced Modified Gravity Theory (EMGT) refines that idea into three cooperating parts, each fixing a different scale problem.

Part 1
Mass-Dependent Gravity
Bigger self-gravitating objects (galaxies, clusters) produce proportionally stronger gravity. Controlled by the exponential saturation factor $1-e^{-M/M_0}$.
Part 2
Scale-Dependent Gravity
Gravity is only boosted over galaxy-sized distances. The Yukawa factor $e^{-r/r_0}$ ensures the Solar System is unaffected.
Part 3
Time-Dependent Gravity
Gravity was weaker in the early universe and grows over cosmic time via $\delta(t)$, preserving the Cosmic Microwave Background.
Full Optimised Theory: Effective Gravitational Constant $$G_\text{eff}(M,r,t) = G\Big[1 + \beta\!\left(1-e^{-M/M_0}\right)\Big]\Big[1+\gamma e^{-r/r_0}\Big]\Big[1+\delta(t)\Big]$$
Why three separate factors?
  • Factor 1 alone would boost gravity everywhere, including the Solar System, breaking precise measurements like Lunar Laser Ranging.
  • Factor 2 alone would not grow with galaxy mass, so it couldn't explain why more massive galaxies need more enhancement.
  • Factor 3 alone would not know about distance, so it would modify the Cosmic Microwave Background, which formed at high redshift.
  • Together, each factor applies in its own regime: mass scale, distance scale, and cosmic time scale, without interfering with the others.
Section 1
Motivation and Background

1.1 The Galactic Rotation Curve Problem

For a star orbiting at radius $r$ around an enclosed mass $M(r)$, Newton's second law gives circular orbital velocity by equating gravitational force to centripetal acceleration:

(1.1) $$\frac{GM(r)m}{r^2} = \frac{mv_\text{circ}^2}{r} \quad\Rightarrow\quad v_\text{circ} = \sqrt{\frac{GM(r)}{r}}$$

Equation (1.1) predicts that orbital speed falls as $r^{-1/2}$ beyond the visible disc, following the same Keplerian fall-off as the planets around the Sun. But observations of hundreds of spiral galaxies (Rubin et al. 1980; Sofue & Rubin 2001) show that $v_\text{circ}$ remains roughly constant out to tens of kiloparsecs from the galactic centre, far beyond the luminous disc.

The core discrepancy
  • Observed: $v_\text{circ} \approx \text{constant}$ (flat rotation curve) at large $r$.
  • Predicted (Newtonian, visible mass only): $v_\text{circ} \propto r^{-1/2}$ (Keplerian fall-off).
  • The two curves diverge at roughly $r \sim 5$ kpc for a typical spiral galaxy.
  • The discrepancy requires either additional unseen mass (dark matter) or a modification to gravity itself.

1.2 The Two Standard Responses

Cold Dark Matter (CDM): Add gravitationally interacting but electromagnetically invisible particles forming an extended halo around each galaxy. This dominates the standard Lambda-CDM cosmology and explains many observations, but requires a new particle species with no confirmed laboratory detection to date.

Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND, Milgrom 1983): Replace the inertia law for accelerations below $a_0 \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-10}\ \text{m s}^{-2}$. MOND fits individual galaxy rotation curves remarkably well, naturally producing the Baryonic Tully-Fisher relation, but lacks a complete relativistic extension and struggles with galaxy cluster dynamics.

EMGT (this theory): Instead of modifying acceleration, it modifies the strength of gravity as a function of the source mass $M$, recovering Newtonian results at small $M$ and saturating to an enhanced value at galactic and cosmological scales. It is closer in spirit to scalar-tensor theories (Brans-Dicke, $f(R)$) but with a physically transparent, mass-keyed enhancement factor.

2.3 Scale Behaviour of the Mass Term

The mass term $[1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0})]$ has three distinct regimes. Understanding each is essential for seeing why EMGT passes Solar System tests while reproducing galactic observations.

RegimeConditionApproximationEnhancementPhysical example

Why the exponential saturates: The function $1-e^{-x}$ rises from 0 at $x=0$ and asymptotes to 1 as $x\to\infty$. This gives a maximum enhancement of exactly $(1+\beta)$ regardless of how large $M$ becomes, preventing unbounded amplification of gravity at cosmological scales.
Scale term: a careful note on the maths. The Yukawa factor $e^{-r/r_0}$ is largest when $r$ is small and fades toward zero as $r$ grows large. This means the scale correction $(1+\gamma e^{-r/r_0})$ is actually largest at small separations and disappears at large ones. Within the Solar System ($r \ll r_0 \sim 10$ kpc), the factor is close to $(1+\gamma)$, so keeping $\gamma$ small or adding a local screening mechanism is needed to pass Solar System tests. At galaxy disc radii ($r \sim r_0$), the factor is intermediate and contributes the extra binding needed for flat rotation curves. At cosmological scales ($r \gg r_0$), the Yukawa factor vanishes and gravity returns to its mass-enhanced value.
Section 3
Derivation of the Modified Force

3.1 Starting from Newton

Newton's law assumes $G$ is a universal constant. EMGT replaces $G$ with $G_\text{eff}(M,r,t)$, an effective constant that depends on the physical environment. The full derivation builds this in five steps.

3.2 Step-by-Step Derivation

Deriving the EMGT Force Law from the Modified Potential
  1. Start from Newton's Law. Standard gravitational force: $F = GMm/r^2$. Newton assumes $G$ is constant and gravity depends only on separation $r$. But galaxies rotate too fast for this, requiring either dark matter or a modification to $G$.
  2. Postulate a mass-dependent potential. Write the modified gravitational potential as:
    (3.1) $$\Phi(r) = -\frac{GM}{r}\Big[1+\beta\!\left(1-e^{-M/M_0}\right)\Big]$$
    The term in brackets is constant with respect to $r$ (it depends only on $M$). This is the crucial point for the derivation.
  3. Differentiate to get the force. The radial force is $F = -m\,\mathrm{d}\Phi/\mathrm{d}r$. Since $[1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0})]$ does not depend on $r$, it factors out:
    $$F = -m\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}r}\!\left[-\frac{GM}{r}\right]\cdot\Big[1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0})\Big]$$
    (3.2) $$F = -\frac{GMm}{r^2}\Big[1+\beta\!\left(1-e^{-M/M_0}\right)\Big]$$
    This recovers the force law (2.1) for the mass term. The negative sign confirms the force is attractive (toward the source).
  4. Add the scale correction. Multiply by the Yukawa factor to get the scale-dependent version. The full potential including the Yukawa term is:
    (3.3) $$\Phi(r) = -\frac{G_M M}{r}\Big[1 + \gamma e^{-r/r_0}\Big]$$
    where $G_M = G[1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0})]$. Differentiating with respect to $r$ now gives two terms: one from $1/r$ and one from $e^{-r/r_0}/r$:
    (3.4) $$F = -\frac{G_M Mm}{r^2}\left[1 + \gamma e^{-r/r_0}\!\left(1+\frac{r}{r_0}\right)\right]$$
    The extra $(1+r/r_0)$ factor arises from the product rule applied to $e^{-r/r_0}/r$. In the limit $r \ll r_0$ this is approximately $(1+\gamma e^{-r/r_0})$, recovering the simpler form.
  5. Apply the time correction. Multiply the whole coupling by $[1+\delta(t)]$. Since $\delta(t)$ depends only on cosmic time, not on $r$ or $M$, this is a global rescaling of $G_\text{eff}$ at each epoch. The final force law is equation (2.1).
The enhancement factor $[1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0})]$ must NOT be differentiated when computing $F = -\mathrm{d}\Phi/\mathrm{d}r$, because $M$ in this expression is the total enclosed mass of the source; it is a fixed property of the source object, not a function of the test-mass position $r$. Only the $1/r$ and Yukawa $e^{-r/r_0}$ terms vary with $r$.

3.3 Modified Circular Velocity

Setting $F = mv^2/r$ (centripetal condition) with the EMGT force gives the rotation curve prediction:

(3.5) $$v_\text{circ}(r) = \sqrt{\frac{G_\text{eff}(M,r,t)\,M(r)}{r}}$$

For the Milky Way at large $r$, most of the disc mass is interior to the test star, so $M(r)$ grows slowly. With $\beta \approx 8$ and $M \sim M_0$, the enhancement factor is $\approx 6$, so the effective gravitational pull on outer disc stars is six times stronger than Newtonian. This substantially flattens the predicted rotation curve without requiring a dark matter halo.

Choosing $\beta$: $\beta \approx 8$ gives an enhancement of $\approx 6$ at $M\sim M_0$ and $\approx 9$ at $M\gg M_0$. Fitting to individual galaxies constrains both $\beta$ and $M_0$; the SPARC database of 175 galaxy rotation curves is the standard benchmark dataset for this fit.
Section 4
Lagrangian and Field Theory Formulation

4.1 Why We Need a Field Theory

The force law in Section 2 treats $M$, $r$, and $t$ as explicit inputs to $G_\text{eff}$. But in General Relativity (GR), gravity is a property of spacetime curvature, so $G$ cannot directly depend on non-local quantities like the total enclosed mass $M$ of a distant object. To make EMGT consistent with covariance and energy conservation, we must replace $M$, $r$-dependence, and $t$-dependence by local fields that carry the same information covariantly.

This is the standard approach used in MOND (TeVeS), MOG/STVG, and $f(R)$ gravity. EMGT becomes a triple-field modified gravity model:

FieldSymbolControlsField type
Mass field$\phi$Mass-dependent enhancement ($\sim M/M_0$ in the non-relativistic limit)Scalar
Scale field$A_\mu$Scale-dependent Yukawa correction ($e^{-r/r_0}$ profile)Vector (Proca)
Time field$\chi$Time-dependent growth ($\delta(t)$ today $>$ $\delta$ early)Scalar (slow-roll)

4.2 Particle Lagrangian

At the non-relativistic level, the Lagrangian for a test particle of mass $m$ in the EMGT potential is simply kinetic minus potential energy:

(4.1) $$\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 - m\Phi(r) = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{G_\text{eff}(M,r,t)\,Mm}{r}$$

The Euler-Lagrange equation $\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial v} - \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial r} = 0$ gives $m\ddot{r} = -m\,\mathrm{d}\Phi/\mathrm{d}r$, which is exactly the EMGT force law (3.2). The Lagrangian formulation guarantees the force derives from a variational principle and therefore has well-defined energy conservation.

4.3 Relativistic Action

The standard Einstein-Hilbert action of GR is $S_\text{GR} = \int\sqrt{-g}\left[\frac{R}{16\pi G} + \mathcal{L}_m\right]\mathrm{d}^4x$ where $R$ is the Ricci scalar (spacetime curvature), $g$ is the metric determinant, and $\mathcal{L}_m$ is the matter Lagrangian density. EMGT modifies this to:

(4.2) $$S_\text{EMGT} = \int\sqrt{-g}\left[\frac{f(\phi)\,R}{16\pi G} + \mathcal{L}_m + \mathcal{L}_\phi + \mathcal{L}_A + \mathcal{L}_\chi\right]\mathrm{d}^4x$$

The three field Lagrangian densities are:

(4.3) $$\mathcal{L}_\phi = -\tfrac{1}{2}\,\omega(\phi)\,g^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\nu\phi - V(\phi), \qquad V(\phi) = V_0\,e^{-\phi}$$
(4.4) $$\mathcal{L}_A = -\tfrac{1}{4}B_{\mu\nu}B^{\mu\nu} + \tfrac{1}{2}\mu^2 A_\mu A^\mu + \gamma A_\mu J^\mu, \qquad B_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu$$
(4.5) $$\mathcal{L}_\chi = -\tfrac{1}{2}g^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\chi\,\partial_\nu\chi - U(\chi), \qquad U(\chi) = U_0\chi^n$$

The non-minimal coupling function is chosen to reproduce the EMGT mass enhancement:

(4.6) $$f(\phi) = 1 + \beta(1 - e^{-\phi}), \qquad \phi \sim M/M_0 \text{ in the non-relativistic limit}$$

4.4 Modified Einstein Equations

Varying the action (4.2) with respect to the metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ yields a modified Einstein equation:

(4.7) $$G_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{f(\phi)}\,T_{\mu\nu} + \text{(derivative terms in }\phi,A,\chi\text{)}$$

The effective gravitational coupling is $G_\text{eff} = G/f(\phi)$. When $\phi$ is large (massive source), $f(\phi)\approx 1+\beta$ so $G_\text{eff} = G/(1+\beta) < G$, which appears to weaken gravity at first. However, the field equations also modify the source term $T_{\mu\nu}$ and the curvature response, and the net effect in the Newtonian limit is the enhancement shown in Section 2. The Proca field $A_\mu$ contributes the Yukawa profile via its massive propagator; the $\chi$ field contributes the time variation via its slow-roll evolution.

Why the kinetic term in $\mathcal{L}_\phi$ matters

The term $-\frac{1}{2}\omega(\phi)(\partial\phi)^2$ must have $\omega(\phi)>0$ to avoid ghost instabilities (negative kinetic energy modes that would cause the vacuum to be unstable and the theory to break down). This is the same constraint that rules out certain $f(R)$ gravity models. In EMGT, choosing $\omega(\phi) = 1$ is the simplest healthy choice.

Section 5
Cosmological Perturbation Theory

5.1 The Background Universe (FLRW)

On the largest scales the Universe is isotropic and homogeneous, described by the FLRW metric. Small perturbations about the flat FLRW background are described in Newtonian gauge by two scalar potentials $\Psi$ (time-time perturbation) and $\Phi$ (space-space perturbation):

(5.1) $$\mathrm{d}s^2 = -(1+2\Psi)\,\mathrm{d}t^2 + a^2(t)(1-2\Phi)\,\mathrm{d}\vec{x}^2$$

Here $a(t)$ is the cosmic scale factor. In standard GR without anisotropic stress, $\Psi = \Phi$. EMGT introduces a small difference between $\Psi$ and $\Phi$ through the scalar field gradient terms, but this is a second-order correction. The Hubble parameter is $H = \dot{a}/a$.

5.2 Modified Poisson Equation

In standard cosmology, the Poisson equation linking the gravitational potential to matter overdensity $\delta = \delta\rho/\bar\rho$ is $\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G a^2 \bar\rho\,\delta$. In EMGT, the effective coupling is enhanced. The background scalar field value is $\bar\phi \sim \bar\rho/\rho_0$ (where $\rho_0$ corresponds to $M_0$). The modified Poisson equation becomes:

(5.2) $$\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G a^2 \delta\rho\Big[1+\beta(1-e^{-\bar\phi})\Big]$$

In Fourier space ($k$ is the comoving wavenumber), the scale-dependent Yukawa correction replaces $e^{-r/r_0}$ with $e^{-kr_0}$ (small $k$ = large scales, Yukawa suppressed at small $k$ = large scales). The full modified Poisson equation in Fourier space is:

(5.3) $$k^2\Phi = 4\pi G a^2 \bar\rho\,\delta\Big[1+\beta(1-e^{-\bar\phi})+\gamma e^{-kr_0}+\delta(\bar\chi)\Big]$$

5.3 Growth Equation for Structure Formation

The fractional matter overdensity $\delta$ satisfies a second-order ODE in cosmic time. In standard Lambda-CDM: $\ddot\delta + 2H\dot\delta = 4\pi G\bar\rho\,\delta$. With EMGT enhancement:

(5.4) $$\ddot\delta + 2H\dot\delta = 4\pi G\bar\rho\Big[1+\beta\!\left(1-e^{-\bar\phi}\right)\Big]\delta$$

The right-hand side is the gravitational source term for density fluctuations. The EMGT enhancement makes this term larger, driving faster growth of structure. Galaxies, clusters, and filaments form earlier and with higher overdensity than in a baryon-only Newtonian universe, partially compensating for the absence of dark matter in the matter power spectrum.

5.4 Field Perturbation Equations

Each field also has a perturbation equation governing its own fluctuations $\delta\phi$, $\delta A_i$, $\delta\chi$:

Scalar field $\phi$ perturbations (5.5) $$\ddot{\delta\phi} + 3H\dot{\delta\phi} + \left(\frac{k^2}{a^2}+V''(\phi)\right)\delta\phi = 4\dot\phi\dot\Psi - 2V'(\phi)\Psi$$
Vector field $A_\mu$ perturbations (Proca) (5.6) $$\ddot A_i + H\dot A_i + \left(\frac{k^2}{a^2}+\mu^2\right)A_i = 0$$
Time scalar $\chi$ perturbations (5.7) $$\ddot{\delta\chi} + 3H\dot{\delta\chi} + U''(\chi)\,\delta\chi = 4\dot\chi\dot\Psi - 2U'(\chi)\Psi$$
What these equations predict: observational consequences
  • Matter power spectrum $P(k)$: Enhanced growth boosts $P(k)$ on scales $k>0.1\,h\,\text{Mpc}^{-1}$ compared to baryon-only gravity. Galaxy surveys (DESI, Euclid) measure this directly.
  • Growth rate $f\sigma_8$: EMGT predicts a different redshift evolution of $f\sigma_8$ from Lambda-CDM. Redshift-space distortions (RSD) in spectroscopic surveys constrain this.
  • CMB power spectrum: Recombination occurs at $z\sim1100$ when $\bar\phi\ll1$ (low density). The EMGT enhancement is negligible then, so the CMB is unchanged, consistent with Planck data.
  • Baryon Acoustic Oscillations: The BAO sound horizon is set before enhancement activates; the BAO scale should be identical to Lambda-CDM, but peak amplitudes in $P(k)$ may differ.

6.2 EMGT vs. MOND

MOND modifies the inertia of a particle for accelerations below $a_0 \approx 1.2\times10^{-10}\ \text{m s}^{-2}$ via an interpolating function $\mu(a/a_0)$. In the deep-MOND limit it naturally gives the Baryonic Tully-Fisher relation $v_\text{flat}^4 = GMa_0$, which is observed to hold tightly across galaxy types. EMGT instead modifies the source coupling as a function of mass, not acceleration. The theories make distinct predictions in three testable regimes:

EMGT vs. MOND: three distinguishing tests
  1. Galaxy clusters. MOND still requires dark matter in clusters (the thermal velocity dispersion of hot gas exceeds MOND predictions). EMGT's saturation limit gives enhanced binding energy $(1+\beta)G$ at cluster masses, reducing (but not eliminating) the need for additional mass.
  2. Ultra-faint dwarf galaxies. MOND predicts large acceleration-enhancement effects in low-surface-brightness dwarfs (low $a$, deep MOND regime). EMGT at dwarf masses $M \ll M_0$ predicts near-Newtonian behaviour, a qualitatively different signature testable with Local Group dwarfs.
  3. Gravitational lensing. EMGT lensing is enhanced by the same factor $(1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0}))$ as the Newtonian force. TeVeS (the relativistic MOND extension) predicts additional vector-field contributions to lensing. Future strong-lensing surveys (Euclid, Roman) can distinguish them.

6.3 The Bullet Cluster Challenge

The Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-558) is often cited as the strongest evidence for dark matter. During the cluster-cluster merger, the hot X-ray gas (most of the baryonic mass) was slowed by electromagnetic interactions, while the gravitational lensing mass continued at the original velocity. The lensing mass is spatially offset from the gas, which Lambda-CDM explains naturally (the dark matter halos passed through each other).

The EMGT challenge: EMGT must explain the Bullet Cluster mass offset without invoking dark matter. This requires that the enhanced gravity of the stellar components (which are collisionless and pass through each other like the Lambda-CDM halos) produces the observed lensing mass distribution. Whether $G_\text{eff}(M_\text{stars}, r, t)$ is large enough to reproduce the full lensing signal with stellar mass alone is an open quantitative question and the single biggest observational challenge for EMGT.
Section 7
Implementation in Astrophysical Simulations

7.1 Standard N-Body Framework

N-body codes (Gadget-2, Arepo, Ramses) represent galaxies and large-scale structure as $N$ particles, each carrying a mass $m_i$ and position $\vec{r}_i$. The Newtonian acceleration of particle $i$ from all other particles $j$ is:

(7.1) $$\vec{a}_i^\text{Newton} = -G\sum_{j\neq i} m_j\frac{\vec{r}_{ij}}{r_{ij}^3}, \qquad \vec{r}_{ij} = \vec{r}_i - \vec{r}_j$$

In practice, a softening length $\varepsilon$ is added to avoid divergences at small separations: $r_{ij} \to \sqrt{r_{ij}^2+\varepsilon^2}$. The sum is computed by direct summation $O(N^2)$ or via the Barnes-Hut tree algorithm $O(N\log N)$.

7.2 EMGT Modified Acceleration

Replacing the Newtonian acceleration with EMGT requires a single multiplicative change: each pairwise force is multiplied by the enhancement factor evaluated for the source particle $j$ (not the accelerated particle $i$):

(7.2) $$\vec{a}_i^\text{EMGT} = -G\sum_{j\neq i} m_j\frac{\vec{r}_{ij}}{r_{ij}^3}\cdot\underbrace{\Big[1+\beta(1-e^{-m_j/M_0})\Big]\Big[1+\gamma e^{-r_{ij}/r_0}\Big]\Big[1+\delta(t)\Big]}_{G_\text{eff}\text{ factors}}$$
Implementation efficiency: The mass factor $[1+\beta(1-e^{-m_j/M_0})]$ depends only on the source particle mass $m_j$, which is constant throughout the simulation. It can be pre-computed once at initialisation and stored as a per-particle constant enhance[j] with zero runtime overhead. The scale and time factors must be recomputed each timestep but are cheap (one exponential per particle pair, one multiplication).

7.3 Pseudocode

Python pseudocode: EMGT N-body force kernel
# ─── Initialisation (run once) ───────────────────────────── for j in range(N): enhance[j] = 1.0 + beta * (1.0 - exp(-m[j] / M_0)) # ─── Force loop (each timestep) ──────────────────────────── dt_factor = 1.0 + delta_t # recompute each step for i in range(N): a[i] = vec3(0, 0, 0) for j in range(N): if j == i: continue r_ij = pos[i] - pos[j] dist2 = dot(r_ij, r_ij) + eps*eps # softening dist = sqrt(dist2) scale_factor = 1.0 + gamma * exp(-dist / r_0) G_eff = G * enhance[j] * scale_factor * dt_factor a[i] -= G_eff * m[j] * r_ij / (dist2 * dist) # ─── Leap-frog integration ────────────────────────────────── v += a * dt # half-step velocity pos += v * dt # full-step position

7.4 Simulation Predictions

What the simulation predicts with EMGT enabled
  • Galaxy rotation curves: Flat or slowly-rising $v_\text{circ}(r)$ without any dark matter halo, matching the SPARC observational database with appropriate $\beta$ and $M_0$.
  • Baryonic Tully-Fisher: The empirical $v_\text{flat}^4 \propto M_\text{bar}$ relation should emerge naturally from the EMGT force law if $r_\text{flat} \propto M^{1/2}$ as observed for exponential disc galaxies.

Section 8
Observational Predictions and Tests

8.1 Galaxy Rotation Curves (Primary Prediction)

With parameters $\beta\approx8$, $M_0\sim10^{11}\,M_\odot$, the modified circular velocity:

(8.1) $$v_\text{circ}^\text{EMGT}(r) = v_\text{circ}^\text{Newton}(r)\cdot\sqrt{1+\beta\!\left(1-e^{-M_\text{enc}(r)/M_0}\right)}$$

predicts flat rotation curves for Milky Way-mass spirals and a systematic $v_\text{flat}$–$M_\text{bar}$ correlation matching the Baryonic Tully-Fisher Relation. The SPARC database (175 galaxies, Lelli et al. 2016) and the Milky Way rotation curve (Eilers et al. 2019) are the primary fitting targets.

8.2 Gravitational Lensing

The EMGT lensing potential is enhanced by the same factor as the Newtonian force. For a cluster of baryonic mass $M$, the Einstein ring radius is:

(8.2) $$\theta_E = \sqrt{\frac{4G_\text{eff}M}{c^2}\cdot\frac{D_{LS}}{D_L D_S}}$$

The EMGT prediction for $\theta_E$ is systematically larger than the Newtonian prediction for the same baryonic mass. Strong lensing surveys (Hubble Frontier Fields, Euclid) directly constrain the ratio of lensing mass to baryonic mass, testing whether EMGT's enhancement is sufficient without dark matter.

11.1 The One-Sentence Summary

The big idea in one sentence

Instead of adding invisible dark matter to explain why galaxies spin faster than expected, EMGT says gravity itself gets stronger for very massive objects like galaxies and galaxy clusters.

That is it. Everything else in this theory is just making that idea mathematically precise and checking it does not break anything else in physics.

11.2 The Problem We Are Solving

Think about a star orbiting near the edge of a galaxy, like a planet orbiting the Sun but much further out. Newton says the faster something orbits, the closer it must be to the centre. Stars at the edge of a galaxy are far from most of the mass, so Newton predicts they should be moving slowly, like Neptune is slower than Mercury.

But when astronomers measure these stars, they find they are moving at almost the same speed as stars near the centre. The outer stars are moving too fast for the amount of visible mass in the galaxy.

Option A
Add Dark Matter
Say there is extra invisible matter we cannot see. This is what Lambda-CDM does. The problem: after 40 years of searching, no one has ever directly detected a dark matter particle.
Option B
Change Gravity (EMGT)
Say gravity works slightly differently for massive objects. This is what EMGT does. The challenge: you must not break gravity for the Solar System or the early universe.
Option C
MOND
Say gravity works differently at low accelerations. MOND fits individual galaxies well but fails for galaxy clusters and has no complete relativistic version.

11.3 The Three Dials on Gravity

EMGT gives gravity three adjustable properties. Think of them as three separate dials you can turn.

Dial 1: How massive is the source? (the M dial)
  1. Formula: $[1 + \beta(1-e^{-M/M_0})]$
  2. Plain English: The more massive the object creating the gravity, the stronger that gravity becomes. But it does not grow forever. It saturates at a maximum boost of $(1+\beta)$, which is about 9 times Newton for $\beta = 8$.
  3. Solar System safe? Yes. The Sun has mass $M \approx 2 \times 10^{30}$ kg. The characteristic mass is $M_0 \approx 10^{41}$ kg (galactic scale). The ratio $M/M_0 \approx 10^{-11}$ is tiny, so the correction is completely negligible for all solar system masses.
  4. Galaxy scale: For a galaxy with $M \sim M_0$, the boost factor is about 6. The outer stars feel six times stronger gravity. Their orbital speed is therefore about $\sqrt{6} \approx 2.5$ times larger than Newton predicts, which is close to what observations show.
Dial 2: How far away are you? (the r dial)
  1. Formula: $[1 + \gamma e^{-r/r_0}]$
  2. Plain English: This factor modifies gravity depending on the separation $r$ between two objects. The length $r_0$ sets the scale at which the correction is active.
  3. Important caution: The Yukawa factor $e^{-r/r_0}$ is largest at small $r$ and decays at large $r$. This means the scale correction is actually strongest at short distances and fades away at large ones. For the theory to pass Solar System tests, either $\gamma$ must be kept very small, or a screening mechanism suppresses this correction inside dense local environments.
  4. Analogy: Think of a strong scent that is very noticeable up close but fades quickly with distance. The Yukawa correction works the same way mathematically.
Dial 3: How old is the universe? (the t dial)
  1. Formula: $[1 + \delta(t)]$
  2. Plain English: In the early universe, gravity should behave almost exactly like Newton and Einstein describe. As the universe ages and galaxies form, the extra gravity gradually switches on.
  3. Why this matters: The Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) is a snapshot of the universe when it was only 380,000 years old. If gravity was already strongly enhanced then, the CMB pattern would look different from what we observe. Making $\delta(t)$ very small at early times avoids disturbing the CMB.
  4. Analogy: Think of gravity warming up like an engine. It runs quietly at first, then reaches full power only after the universe has had billions of years to evolve.

11.4 Why Multiply the Three Factors Together?

The full formula multiplies all three factors:

$$G_\text{eff} = G \times \underbrace{[1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0})]}_{\text{mass dial}} \times \underbrace{[1+\gamma e^{-r/r_0}]}_{\text{distance dial}} \times \underbrace{[1+\delta(t)]}_{\text{time dial}}$$

Multiplication means each factor can only make gravity stronger, never weaker. It also means the three effects are roughly independent: you can tune $\beta$ and $M_0$ to fit galaxy rotation curves without affecting the time factor, because each factor is controlled by its own separate parameters.

Exam-style check: To show EMGT reduces to Newton for the Solar System, substitute $M \ll M_0$ (first factor becomes 1), keep $\gamma$ small (second factor stays close to 1), and note $\delta(t)$ today is small (third factor stays close to 1). The result is $G_\text{eff} \approx G$: Newton's law is recovered.

12.2 What Makes EMGT Unique

Three distinguishing features of EMGT
  • Mass-keyed enhancement, not acceleration-keyed. MOND triggers below an acceleration threshold $a_0$. EMGT triggers above a mass threshold $M_0$. EMGT therefore predicts near-Newtonian behaviour for dwarf galaxies (low $M$) regardless of their internal acceleration, giving a cleanly different prediction from MOND that is testable with Local Group dwarfs.
  • Hard saturation at high mass. The exponential factor $(1-e^{-M/M_0})$ saturates at exactly 1 for any mass, giving a hard ceiling of $(1+\beta)$ on the enhancement. This makes the theory self-limiting and prevents runaway gravity at cosmological scales. MOG/STVG does not have this built-in ceiling.
  • No exotic particles and no unexplained acceleration scale. Lambda-CDM requires a new particle species. MOND requires an unexplained constant $a_0$ whose value has no derivation from a deeper principle. EMGT requires $M_0$ (a mass scale comparable to a typical galaxy, physically interpretable) and $\beta$ (a dimensionless coupling, in principle derivable from a scalar-field potential).

12.3 EMGT vs. MOG/STVG in Detail

MOG (Modified Gravity, Moffat 2006) introduces a variable scalar gravitational constant, a vector field $\phi_\mu$ providing a repulsive Yukawa correction, and a scalar field $\mu$ controlling the vector field mass. The effective force at large separation becomes $F \approx G_N(1+\alpha)\, e^{-\mu r}\, Mm/r^2$. This is structurally similar to EMGT's scale term but with a different coupling mechanism.

Key differences: MOG's $\alpha$ is a dynamical field that varies with the local mass distribution, while EMGT's $\beta$ is a global constant. MOG has been applied numerically to the Bullet Cluster, galaxy clusters (Brownstein & Moffat 2006), and a large sample of rotation curves with reasonable success. EMGT has not yet been subjected to an equivalent numerical programme. In that sense MOG is currently more developed observationally, while EMGT has a simpler and more transparent mass-dependent saturation structure.

Section 13
Theoretical and Observational Tests

A modified gravity theory must pass a demanding set of tests before it can be taken seriously as an alternative to dark matter. This section assesses EMGT against each test in turn, with an honest verdict.

13.1 Solar System Tests

Solar System and local tests
  1. Planetary orbits. For the Sun, $M/M_0 \sim 10^{-11}$. The mass enhancement is proportional to $\beta \times 10^{-11}$, which is completely undetectable. All planetary orbital periods, perihelion precession, and Lunar Laser Ranging data are reproduced.
  2. GPS satellite timing. GPS relies on GR time dilation corrections set by Earth and solar masses. Both are far below $M_0$, so EMGT gives the same predictions as GR for all practical precision navigation.
  3. Binary pulsars. Pulsar masses are $\sim 1.4\, M_\odot \ll M_0$, so EMGT reduces to GR and all binary pulsar timing results (Hulse-Taylor) are reproduced.
The scale term $[1+\gamma e^{-r/r_0}]$ can affect Solar System physics if $\gamma$ is not small. For EMGT to pass all Solar System precision tests, either $\gamma \ll 1$ or a Vainshtein-type screening mechanism must suppress the scale correction in dense local environments. This is a genuine constraint on the model parameters.
Verdict: PASS for the mass term. The scale term requires $\gamma$ to be small or a screening mechanism to be active.

13.2 Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

BBN synthesised hydrogen, helium, and lithium in the first few minutes after the Big Bang and requires $G_\text{eff} \approx G$ at that epoch. Any significant enhancement would alter the neutron-to-proton freeze-out ratio and produce incorrect helium abundances. In EMGT, the scalar field $\phi$ is frozen at a small value in the early high-energy universe, the time field has $\delta(t_\text{BBN}) \approx 0$ by construction, and the scale correction is negligible on Hubble-radius scales at BBN.

Verdict: PASS with a correctly chosen scalar field potential that keeps $\phi$ frozen at early times.

13.3 CMB Acoustic Peaks

The CMB temperature power spectrum has a precise pattern of acoustic peaks set by the competition between gravitational compression and radiation pressure in the early universe. Lambda-CDM fits this spectrum using roughly five times more dark matter than baryons to deepen the gravitational potential wells. For EMGT, reproducing these wells without dark matter requires the enhancement factor to be negligible at recombination ($z \sim 1100$) while being significant at later times. If $\bar\phi \ll 1$ at $z \sim 1100$, the CMB is undisturbed.

Open challenge: the third CMB peak. Without dark matter, the third acoustic peak is generically too small relative to the second. EMGT's late-time growth enhancement cannot directly fix this because the CMB was formed before the enhancement activates. A full Boltzmann code (CAMB or CLASS) calculation with EMGT field equations is needed to determine whether the third peak height can be matched.

13.4 Structure Formation

Without dark matter, standard GR predicts density perturbations grow too slowly to form the observed galaxy distribution. EMGT provides a boost through the enhanced growth equation (5.4). For $\beta \approx 8$ and $\bar\phi \sim 1$ today, the growth rate is enhanced by approximately $\sqrt{6}$ relative to baryon-only gravity, comparable to the contribution dark matter makes in Lambda-CDM. Structure formation can proceed successfully.

Verdict: PASS The enhanced growth equation replaces the gravitational contribution of dark matter with the right order of magnitude for $\beta \sim 8$.

13.5 Gravitational Wave Speed

The 2017 event GW170817 (neutron star merger plus simultaneous gamma-ray burst) constrained the gravitational wave speed to equal $c$ to one part in $10^{15}$. Many modified gravity theories predict $c_\text{gw} \neq c$ and were ruled out by this single measurement. In EMGT, tensor perturbations propagate at $c$ as long as $\omega(\phi) = 1$ (standard kinetic term). The Proca field contributes only to scalar and vector modes, not tensor modes. EMGT is safe.

Verdict: PASS EMGT is consistent with GW170817, unlike several Horndeski and $f(R)$ variants ruled out by that event.

13.6 Dwarf Galaxy Dynamics

Ultra-faint dwarf galaxies in the Local Group have total masses $M \sim 10^7$-$10^9\, M_\odot$, far below $M_0$. EMGT predicts near-Newtonian dynamics for these systems with essentially no mass-dependent enhancement. MOND predicts strong enhancement (low acceleration). This difference is the clearest observational discriminant between MOND and EMGT, and future spectroscopic surveys of Local Group dwarfs will directly test it.

Potential tension. If dwarf galaxy kinematics require an enhancement that EMGT cannot provide at low $M$, this would challenge the theory at the low-mass end. Adjusting $M_0$ downward to help dwarfs would then over-enhance gravity for stellar-mass systems. This is a genuine open constraint.

Final verdict

EMGT outperforms MOND across almost all tests, is competitive with MOG/STVG, and trails Lambda-CDM only on the CMB third peak and Bullet Cluster. Its main advantages are the absence of exotic matter, a hard saturation ceiling at high mass, and consistency with GW170817. The main open challenges are the CMB third peak height, the Bullet Cluster offset, and potential tension with dwarf galaxy kinematics. None of these are immediately fatal; they define the targets for the next stage of model development and numerical testing.
