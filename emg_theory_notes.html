Lasting Gravitational Effect Theory

A self-consistent, dark-matter-free theory of gravity in which massive, self-gravitating systems accumulate a lasting gravitational effect that grows exponentially with their mass. Covers the full derivation from Newton to Lagrangian field theory, cosmological perturbation equations, and N-body simulation implementation.

Overview
The Big Idea

Instead of adding invisible dark matter to explain why galaxies spin faster than they should, this theory asks: what if gravity leaves a lasting effect that grows exponentially with the mass of an object? The Lasting Gravitational Effect Theory (LGET) refines that idea into three cooperating parts, each fixing a different scale problem.

Part 1
Mass-Proportional Lasting Effect
Massive self-gravitating systems (galaxies, clusters) accumulate a lasting gravitational effect that grows exponentially with their mass. Quantified by the exponential saturation factor $1-e^{-M/M_0}$.
Part 2
Scale-Dependent Lasting Reach
The lasting gravitational effect only extends over galaxy-sized distances. The Yukawa factor $e^{-r/r_0}$ ensures the Solar System is unaffected.
Part 3
Time-Accumulated Gravitational Memory
The lasting effect builds up over cosmic time via $\delta(t)$. In the early universe it was negligible, growing as massive structures formed — preserving the Cosmic Microwave Background.
Full Optimised Theory — Effective Gravitational Coupling (with Lasting Effect) $$G_\text{eff}(M,r,t) = G\Big[1 + \beta\!\left(1-e^{-M/M_0}\right)\Big]\Big[1+\gamma e^{-r/r_0}\Big]\Big[1+\delta(t)\Big]$$
Why three separate factors?
  • Factor 1 alone would give all objects the same lasting gravitational legacy regardless of where they are — including in the Solar System, breaking precise measurements like Lunar Laser Ranging.
  • Factor 2 alone would not grow with galaxy mass — the lasting effect would be the same for a star as for a galaxy cluster.
  • Factor 3 alone would not know about distance — the accumulated effect would modify the Cosmic Microwave Background, which formed at high redshift before massive structures had time to build up their gravitational legacy.
  • Together, each factor applies in its own regime: mass scale, distance scale, and cosmic time scale — without interfering with the others.
Section 1
Motivation and Background

1.1 The Galactic Rotation Curve Problem

For a star orbiting at radius $r$ around an enclosed mass $M(r)$, Newton's second law gives circular orbital velocity by equating gravitational force to centripetal acceleration:

(1.1) $$\frac{GM(r)m}{r^2} = \frac{mv_\text{circ}^2}{r} \quad\Rightarrow\quad v_\text{circ} = \sqrt{\frac{GM(r)}{r}}$$

Equation (1.1) predicts that orbital speed falls as $r^{-1/2}$ beyond the visible disc — the same Keplerian fall-off as the planets around the Sun. But observations of hundreds of spiral galaxies (Rubin et al. 1980; Sofue & Rubin 2001) show that $v_\text{circ}$ remains roughly constant out to tens of kiloparsecs from the galactic centre, far beyond the luminous disc.

The core discrepancy
  • Observed: $v_\text{circ} \approx \text{constant}$ (flat rotation curve) at large $r$.
  • Predicted (Newtonian, visible mass only): $v_\text{circ} \propto r^{-1/2}$ (Keplerian fall-off).
  • The two curves diverge at roughly $r \sim 5$ kpc for a typical spiral galaxy.
  • The discrepancy requires either additional unseen mass (dark matter) or a modification to gravity itself.

1.2 The Two Standard Responses

Cold Dark Matter (CDM): Add gravitationally interacting but electromagnetically invisible particles forming an extended halo around each galaxy. This dominates the standard Lambda-CDM cosmology and explains many observations, but requires a new particle species with no confirmed laboratory detection to date.

Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND, Milgrom 1983): Replace the inertia law for accelerations below $a_0 \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-10}\ \text{m s}^{-2}$. MOND fits individual galaxy rotation curves remarkably well, naturally producing the Baryonic Tully-Fisher relation, but lacks a complete relativistic extension and struggles with galaxy cluster dynamics.

LGET (this theory): Instead of modifying acceleration, LGET proposes that gravity accumulates a lasting effect proportional to the source mass $M$. Small masses — planets and stars — have built up negligible lasting imprint. Galaxies and clusters, with their immense mass, have over cosmic time accumulated a deep gravitational legacy that persists in the field, quantified by the exponential factor $1-e^{-M/M_0}$. The theory recovers Newtonian results at small $M$ and saturates to a maximum lasting effect at galactic and cosmological scales. It is closest in spirit to scalar-tensor theories (Brans-Dicke, $f(R)$) but with a physically transparent, mass-keyed accumulation factor.

2.3 Scale Behaviour of the Mass-Proportional Lasting Effect

The mass term $[1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0})]$ encodes how deeply a gravitating system has imprinted its lasting effect on the surrounding field. It has three distinct regimes. Understanding each is essential for seeing why LGET passes Solar System tests while reproducing galactic observations.

RegimeConditionApproximationEnhancementPhysical example
Small mass$M \ll M_0$$e^{-M/M_0}\approx 1-M/M_0$, so correction $\approx \beta M/M_0$$\approx 1$ (negligible)Sun ($M/M_0\sim 10^{-11}$): no lasting imprint — totally undetectable
Transition$M \sim M_0$$e^{-M/M_0}\approx 0.37$, so factor $\approx 1+0.63\beta$$\approx 6$ for $\beta=8$Milky Way disc: accumulated lasting effect flattens the rotation curve

Why the lasting effect saturates: The function $1-e^{-x}$ rises from 0 at $x=0$ and asymptotes to 1 as $x\to\infty$. This means a gravitating system can accumulate at most $(1+\beta)$ times the baseline gravitational effect — regardless of how large $M$ grows. The lasting imprint has a natural ceiling, preventing unbounded amplification at cosmological scales.
Scale term behaviour: At small $r \ll r_0$, $e^{-r/r_0}\approx 1$ so $G_\text{eff}\approx G(1+\gamma)$ — the lasting gravitational effect is felt fully nearby. At large $r \gg r_0$, $e^{-r/r_0}\to 0$, so the scale contribution disappears. This is a Yukawa-type suppression: the lasting effect has a finite reach on cosmic scales but extends across galactic scales because $r_0\sim 10$–$100\ \text{kpc}$ is chosen to match galaxy sizes.
Section 3
Derivation of the Modified Force

3.1 Starting from Newton

Newton's law assumes $G$ is a universal constant. LGET replaces $G$ with $G_\text{eff}(M,r,t)$ — an effective coupling that carries the lasting gravitational memory of the source. The full derivation builds this in five steps.

3.2 Step-by-Step Derivation

Deriving the LGET Force Law from the Modified Potential
  1. Start from Newton's Law. Standard gravitational force: $F = GMm/r^2$. Newton assumes $G$ is constant and gravity depends only on separation $r$. But galaxies rotate too fast for this, requiring either dark matter or a modification that encodes a lasting effect of mass on the gravitational field.
  2. Postulate a mass-imprinted potential. Write the modified gravitational potential as:
    (3.1) $$\Phi(r) = -\frac{GM}{r}\Big[1+\beta\!\left(1-e^{-M/M_0}\right)\Big]$$
    The term in brackets is constant with respect to $r$ (it depends only on $M$). This is the crucial point for the derivation.
  3. Differentiate to get the force. The radial force is $F = -m\,\mathrm{d}\Phi/\mathrm{d}r$. Since $[1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0})]$ does not depend on $r$, it factors out:
    $$F = -m\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}r}\!\left[-\frac{GM}{r}\right]\cdot\Big[1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0})\Big]$$
    (3.2) $$F = -\frac{GMm}{r^2}\Big[1+\beta\!\left(1-e^{-M/M_0}\right)\Big]$$
    This recovers the force law (2.1) for the mass term. The negative sign confirms the force is attractive (toward the source).
  4. Add the scale correction. Multiply by the Yukawa factor to get the scale-dependent version. The full potential including the Yukawa term is:
    (3.3) $$\Phi(r) = -\frac{G_M M}{r}\Big[1 + \gamma e^{-r/r_0}\Big]$$
    where $G_M = G[1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0})]$. Differentiating with respect to $r$ now gives two terms — one from $1/r$ and one from $e^{-r/r_0}/r$:
    (3.4) $$F = -\frac{G_M Mm}{r^2}\left[1 + \gamma e^{-r/r_0}\!\left(1+\frac{r}{r_0}\right)\right]$$
    The extra $(1+r/r_0)$ factor arises from the product rule applied to $e^{-r/r_0}/r$. In the limit $r \ll r_0$ this is approximately $(1+\gamma e^{-r/r_0})$, recovering the simpler form.
  5. Apply the time correction. Multiply the whole coupling by $[1+\delta(t)]$. Since $\delta(t)$ depends only on cosmic time, not on $r$ or $M$, this is a global rescaling of $G_\text{eff}$ at each epoch. The final force law is equation (2.1).
The lasting-effect factor $[1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0})]$ must NOT be differentiated when computing $F = -\mathrm{d}\Phi/\mathrm{d}r$, because $M$ in this expression is the total enclosed mass of the source — it is a fixed property of the source object encoding its gravitational legacy, not a function of the test-mass position $r$. Only the $1/r$ and Yukawa $e^{-r/r_0}$ terms vary with $r$.

3.3 Modified Circular Velocity

Setting $F = mv^2/r$ (centripetal condition) with the LGET force gives the rotation curve prediction:

(3.5) $$v_\text{circ}(r) = \sqrt{\frac{G_\text{eff}(M,r,t)\,M(r)}{r}}$$

For the Milky Way at large $r$, most of the disc mass is interior to the test star, so $M(r)$ grows slowly. With $\beta \approx 8$ and $M \sim M_0$, the lasting-effect factor is $\approx 6$, so the effective gravitational pull on outer disc stars is six times stronger than Newtonian — a direct consequence of the galaxy's accumulated gravitational legacy. This substantially flattens the predicted rotation curve without requiring a dark matter halo.

Choosing $\beta$: $\beta \approx 8$ gives a lasting-effect factor of $\approx 6$ at $M\sim M_0$ and $\approx 9$ at $M\gg M_0$. Fitting to individual galaxies constrains both $\beta$ and $M_0$; the SPARC database of 175 galaxy rotation curves is the standard benchmark dataset for this fit.
Section 4
Lagrangian and Field Theory Formulation

4.1 Why We Need a Field Theory

The force law in Section 2 treats $M$, $r$, and $t$ as explicit inputs to $G_\text{eff}$. But in General Relativity (GR), gravity is a property of spacetime curvature — $G$ cannot directly depend on non-local quantities like the total enclosed mass $M$ of a distant object. To make LGET consistent with covariance and energy conservation, we must replace $M$, $r$-dependence, and $t$-dependence by local fields that carry the same information covariantly. The lasting gravitational effect is then encoded in the dynamics of these fields, which are sourced by matter and propagate through spacetime.

This is the standard approach used in MOND (TeVeS), MOG/STVG, and $f(R)$ gravity. LGET becomes a triple-field lasting-gravity model:

FieldSymbolControlsField type
Mass field$\phi$Carries the mass-accumulated lasting gravitational imprint ($\sim M/M_0$ in the non-relativistic limit)Scalar
Scale field$A_\mu$Sets the spatial reach of the lasting effect — Yukawa profile ($e^{-r/r_0}$)Vector (Proca)
Time field$\chi$Encodes how the lasting effect has grown over cosmic history ($\delta(t)$ today $>$ $\delta$ early)Scalar (slow-roll)

4.2 Particle Lagrangian

At the non-relativistic level, the Lagrangian for a test particle of mass $m$ in the LGET potential is simply kinetic minus potential energy:

(4.1) $$\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 - m\Phi(r) = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{G_\text{eff}(M,r,t)\,Mm}{r}$$

The Euler-Lagrange equation $\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial v} - \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial r} = 0$ gives $m\ddot{r} = -m\,\mathrm{d}\Phi/\mathrm{d}r$, which is exactly the LGET force law (3.2). The Lagrangian formulation guarantees the lasting-effect force derives from a variational principle and therefore has well-defined energy conservation.

4.3 Relativistic Action

The standard Einstein-Hilbert action of GR is $S_\text{GR} = \int\sqrt{-g}\left[\frac{R}{16\pi G} + \mathcal{L}_m\right]\mathrm{d}^4x$ where $R$ is the Ricci scalar (spacetime curvature), $g$ is the metric determinant, and $\mathcal{L}_m$ is the matter Lagrangian density. LGET modifies this to:

(4.2) $$S_\text{LGET} = \int\sqrt{-g}\left[\frac{f(\phi)\,R}{16\pi G} + \mathcal{L}_m + \mathcal{L}_\phi + \mathcal{L}_A + \mathcal{L}_\chi\right]\mathrm{d}^4x$$

The three field Lagrangian densities are:

(4.3) $$\mathcal{L}_\phi = -\tfrac{1}{2}\,\omega(\phi)\,g^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial_\nu\phi - V(\phi), \qquad V(\phi) = V_0\,e^{-\phi}$$
(4.4) $$\mathcal{L}_A = -\tfrac{1}{4}B_{\mu\nu}B^{\mu\nu} + \tfrac{1}{2}\mu^2 A_\mu A^\mu + \gamma A_\mu J^\mu, \qquad B_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu$$
(4.5) $$\mathcal{L}_\chi = -\tfrac{1}{2}g^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\chi\,\partial_\nu\chi - U(\chi), \qquad U(\chi) = U_0\chi^n$$

The non-minimal coupling function is chosen to reproduce the LGET mass-proportional lasting effect:

(4.6) $$f(\phi) = 1 + \beta(1 - e^{-\phi}), \qquad \phi \sim M/M_0 \text{ in the non-relativistic limit}$$

4.4 Modified Einstein Equations

Varying the action (4.2) with respect to the metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ yields a modified Einstein equation:

(4.7) $$G_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{f(\phi)}\,T_{\mu\nu} + \text{(derivative terms in }\phi,A,\chi\text{)}$$

The effective gravitational coupling is $G_\text{eff} = G/f(\phi)$. When $\phi$ is large (massive source), $f(\phi)\approx 1+\beta$ so $G_\text{eff} = G/(1+\beta) < G$ — which appears to weaken gravity at first. However, the field equations also modify the source term $T_{\mu\nu}$ and the curvature response, and the net effect in the Newtonian limit is the lasting-effect enhancement shown in Section 2. The Proca field $A_\mu$ contributes the Yukawa profile via its massive propagator, setting the spatial reach of the lasting effect; the $\chi$ field contributes the time variation via its slow-roll evolution, encoding how that effect has accumulated over cosmic history.

Why the kinetic term in $\mathcal{L}_\phi$ matters

The term $-\frac{1}{2}\omega(\phi)(\partial\phi)^2$ must have $\omega(\phi)>0$ to avoid ghost instabilities — negative kinetic energy modes that would cause the vacuum to be unstable and the theory to break down. This is the same constraint that rules out certain $f(R)$ gravity models. In LGET, choosing $\omega(\phi) = 1$ is the simplest healthy choice.

Section 5
Cosmological Perturbation Theory

5.1 The Background Universe (FLRW)

On the largest scales the Universe is isotropic and homogeneous, described by the FLRW metric. Small perturbations about the flat FLRW background are described in Newtonian gauge by two scalar potentials $\Psi$ (time-time perturbation) and $\Phi$ (space-space perturbation):

(5.1) $$\mathrm{d}s^2 = -(1+2\Psi)\,\mathrm{d}t^2 + a^2(t)(1-2\Phi)\,\mathrm{d}\vec{x}^2$$

Here $a(t)$ is the cosmic scale factor. In standard GR without anisotropic stress, $\Psi = \Phi$. LGET introduces a small difference between $\Psi$ and $\Phi$ through the scalar field gradient terms, but this is a second-order correction. The Hubble parameter is $H = \dot{a}/a$.

5.2 Modified Poisson Equation

In standard cosmology, the Poisson equation linking the gravitational potential to matter overdensity $\delta = \delta\rho/\bar\rho$ is $\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G a^2 \bar\rho\,\delta$. In LGET, the lasting gravitational effect of the accumulated mass is encoded in the effective coupling. The background scalar field value is $\bar\phi \sim \bar\rho/\rho_0$ (where $\rho_0$ corresponds to $M_0$). The modified Poisson equation becomes:

(5.2) $$\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G a^2 \delta\rho\Big[1+\beta(1-e^{-\bar\phi})\Big]$$

In Fourier space ($k$ is the comoving wavenumber), the scale-dependent Yukawa correction replaces $e^{-r/r_0}$ with $e^{-kr_0}$ (small $k$ = large scales, Yukawa suppressed at small $k$ = large scales). The full modified Poisson equation in Fourier space is:

(5.3) $$k^2\Phi = 4\pi G a^2 \bar\rho\,\delta\Big[1+\beta(1-e^{-\bar\phi})+\gamma e^{-kr_0}+\delta(\bar\chi)\Big]$$

5.3 Growth Equation for Structure Formation

The fractional matter overdensity $\delta$ satisfies a second-order ODE in cosmic time. In standard Lambda-CDM: $\ddot\delta + 2H\dot\delta = 4\pi G\bar\rho\,\delta$. With the LGET lasting-effect factor:

(5.4) $$\ddot\delta + 2H\dot\delta = 4\pi G\bar\rho\Big[1+\beta\!\left(1-e^{-\bar\phi}\right)\Big]\delta$$

The right-hand side is the gravitational source term for density fluctuations. The LGET lasting effect makes this term larger, driving faster growth of structure. Galaxies, clusters, and filaments form earlier and with higher overdensity than in a baryon-only Newtonian universe, partially compensating for the absence of dark matter in the matter power spectrum.

5.4 Field Perturbation Equations

Each field also has a perturbation equation governing its own fluctuations $\delta\phi$, $\delta A_i$, $\delta\chi$:

Scalar field $\phi$ perturbations (5.5) $$\ddot{\delta\phi} + 3H\dot{\delta\phi} + \left(\frac{k^2}{a^2}+V''(\phi)\right)\delta\phi = 4\dot\phi\dot\Psi - 2V'(\phi)\Psi$$
Vector field $A_\mu$ perturbations (Proca) (5.6) $$\ddot A_i + H\dot A_i + \left(\frac{k^2}{a^2}+\mu^2\right)A_i = 0$$
Time scalar $\chi$ perturbations (5.7) $$\ddot{\delta\chi} + 3H\dot{\delta\chi} + U''(\chi)\,\delta\chi = 4\dot\chi\dot\Psi - 2U'(\chi)\Psi$$
What these equations predict — observational consequences
  • Matter power spectrum $P(k)$: The lasting gravitational effect drives faster growth, boosting $P(k)$ on scales $k>0.1\,h\,\text{Mpc}^{-1}$ compared to baryon-only gravity. Galaxy surveys (DESI, Euclid) measure this directly.
  • Growth rate $f\sigma_8$: LGET predicts a different redshift evolution of $f\sigma_8$ from Lambda-CDM. Redshift-space distortions (RSD) in spectroscopic surveys constrain this.
  • CMB power spectrum: Recombination occurs at $z\sim1100$ when $\bar\phi\ll1$ (low density). The LGET lasting effect is negligible then — CMB is unchanged, consistent with Planck data.
  • Baryon Acoustic Oscillations: The BAO sound horizon is set before enhancement activates; the BAO scale should be identical to Lambda-CDM, but peak amplitudes in $P(k)$ may differ.

6.2 LGET vs. MOND

MOND modifies the inertia of a particle for accelerations below $a_0 \approx 1.2\times10^{-10}\ \text{m s}^{-2}$ via an interpolating function $\mu(a/a_0)$. In the deep-MOND limit it naturally gives the Baryonic Tully-Fisher relation $v_\text{flat}^4 = GMa_0$, which is observed to hold tightly across galaxy types. LGET instead proposes that the source object accumulates a lasting gravitational legacy proportional to its mass — modifying the coupling as a function of mass, not acceleration. The theories make distinct predictions in three testable regimes:

LGET vs. MOND: three distinguishing tests
  1. Galaxy clusters. MOND still requires dark matter in clusters (the thermal velocity dispersion of hot gas exceeds MOND predictions). LGET's saturation limit gives an enhanced lasting binding effect of $(1+\beta)G$ at cluster masses — reducing (but not eliminating) the need for additional mass.
  2. Ultra-faint dwarf galaxies. MOND predicts large acceleration-enhancement effects in low-surface-brightness dwarfs (low $a$, deep MOND regime). LGET at dwarf masses $M \ll M_0$ predicts near-Newtonian behaviour, since low-mass objects have accumulated negligible gravitational legacy — a qualitatively different signature testable with Local Group dwarfs.
  3. Gravitational lensing. LGET lensing is enhanced by the same lasting-effect factor $(1+\beta(1-e^{-M/M_0}))$ as the Newtonian force. TeVeS (the relativistic MOND extension) predicts additional vector-field contributions to lensing. Future strong-lensing surveys (Euclid, Roman) can distinguish them.

6.3 The Bullet Cluster Challenge

The Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-558) is often cited as the strongest evidence for dark matter. During the cluster-cluster merger, the hot X-ray gas (most of the baryonic mass) was slowed by electromagnetic interactions, while the gravitational lensing mass continued at the original velocity. The lensing mass is spatially offset from the gas — which Lambda-CDM explains naturally (the dark matter halos passed through each other).

The LGET challenge: LGET must explain the Bullet Cluster mass offset without invoking dark matter. This requires that the lasting gravitational effect of the stellar components (which are collisionless and pass through each other like Lambda-CDM halos) produces the observed lensing mass distribution. Whether $G_\text{eff}(M_\text{stars}, r, t)$ — encoding the full lasting gravitational legacy of those stars — is large enough to reproduce the full lensing signal with stellar mass alone is an open quantitative question, and the single biggest observational challenge for LGET.
Section 7
Implementation in Astrophysical Simulations

7.1 Standard N-Body Framework

N-body codes (Gadget-2, Arepo, Ramses) represent galaxies and large-scale structure as $N$ particles, each carrying a mass $m_i$ and position $\vec{r}_i$. The Newtonian acceleration of particle $i$ from all other particles $j$ is:

(7.1) $$\vec{a}_i^\text{Newton} = -G\sum_{j\neq i} m_j\frac{\vec{r}_{ij}}{r_{ij}^3}, \qquad \vec{r}_{ij} = \vec{r}_i - \vec{r}_j$$

In practice, a softening length $\varepsilon$ is added to avoid divergences at small separations: $r_{ij} \to \sqrt{r_{ij}^2+\varepsilon^2}$. The sum is computed by direct summation $O(N^2)$ or via the Barnes-Hut tree algorithm $O(N\log N)$.

7.2 LGET Modified Acceleration

Replacing the Newtonian acceleration with LGET requires a single multiplicative change: each pairwise force is multiplied by the lasting-effect factor evaluated for the source particle $j$ (not the accelerated particle $i$). This reflects the key physical idea — it is the source object's accumulated gravitational legacy, proportional to its mass, that modifies the force it exerts:

(7.2) $$\vec{a}_i^\text{LGET} = -G\sum_{j\neq i} m_j\frac{\vec{r}_{ij}}{r_{ij}^3}\cdot\underbrace{\Big[1+\beta(1-e^{-m_j/M_0})\Big]\Big[1+\gamma e^{-r_{ij}/r_0}\Big]\Big[1+\delta(t)\Big]}_{G_\text{eff}\text{ — lasting-effect factors}}$$
Implementation efficiency: The lasting-effect mass factor $[1+\beta(1-e^{-m_j/M_0})]$ depends only on the source particle mass $m_j$, which is constant throughout the simulation. It encodes each particle's fixed gravitational legacy and can be pre-computed once at initialisation and stored as a per-particle constant enhance[j] with zero runtime overhead. The scale and time factors must be recomputed each timestep but are cheap (one exponential per particle pair, one multiplication).

7.3 Pseudocode

Python pseudocode — LGET N-body force kernel
# ─── Initialisation (run once) ───────────────────────────── for j in range(N): enhance[j] = 1.0 + beta * (1.0 - exp(-m[j] / M_0)) # ─── Force loop (each timestep) ──────────────────────────── dt_factor = 1.0 + delta_t # recompute each step for i in range(N): a[i] = vec3(0, 0, 0) for j in range(N): if j == i: continue r_ij = pos[i] - pos[j] dist2 = dot(r_ij, r_ij) + eps*eps # softening dist = sqrt(dist2) scale_factor = 1.0 + gamma * exp(-dist / r_0) G_eff = G * enhance[j] * scale_factor * dt_factor a[i] -= G_eff * m[j] * r_ij / (dist2 * dist) # ─── Leap-frog integration ────────────────────────────────── v += a * dt # half-step velocity pos += v * dt # full-step position

7.4 Simulation Predictions

What the simulation predicts with LGET enabled
  • Galaxy rotation curves: Flat or slowly-rising $v_\text{circ}(r)$ without any dark matter halo — matching the SPARC observational database with appropriate $\beta$ and $M_0$.
  • Baryonic Tully-Fisher: The empirical $v_\text{flat}^4 \propto M_\text{bar}$ relation should emerge naturally from the LGET force law — a direct consequence of the mass-proportional lasting effect — if $r_\text{flat} \propto M^{1/2}$ as observed for exponential disc galaxies.
  • Galaxy clusters: Velocity dispersions and lensing mass enhanced by $(1+\beta)$ at cluster mass scales, reducing the required additional mass component.

  • No dark matter halos: Galaxies remain bound and form spiral arms correctly, but no extended dark matter substructure appears in the particle distribution.
Section 8
Observational Predictions and Tests

8.1 Galaxy Rotation Curves (Primary Prediction)

With parameters $\beta\approx8$, $M_0\sim10^{11}\,M_\odot$, the modified circular velocity — shaped by the galaxy's accumulated lasting gravitational effect:

(8.1) $$v_\text{circ}^\text{LGET}(r) = v_\text{circ}^\text{Newton}(r)\cdot\sqrt{1+\beta\!\left(1-e^{-M_\text{enc}(r)/M_0}\right)}$$

predicts flat rotation curves for Milky Way-mass spirals and a systematic $v_\text{flat}$–$M_\text{bar}$ correlation matching the Baryonic Tully-Fisher Relation. The SPARC database (175 galaxies, Lelli et al. 2016) and the Milky Way rotation curve (Eilers et al. 2019) are the primary fitting targets.

8.2 Gravitational Lensing

The LGET lensing potential is enhanced by the same lasting-effect factor as the Newtonian force. For a cluster of baryonic mass $M$, the Einstein ring radius is:

(8.2) $$\theta_E = \sqrt{\frac{4G_\text{eff}M}{c^2}\cdot\frac{D_{LS}}{D_L D_S}}$$

The LGET prediction for $\theta_E$ is systematically larger than the Newtonian prediction for the same baryonic mass, because the lasting gravitational legacy of the cluster's mass amplifies the lensing effect. Strong lensing surveys (Hubble Frontier Fields, Euclid) directly constrain the ratio of lensing mass to baryonic mass, testing whether LGET's lasting effect is sufficient without dark matter.

Learning Objectives

  • State the LGET force law and explain what each of the three multiplicative factors physically represents in terms of a lasting gravitational effect.
  • Derive the modified force from the modified potential by differentiation, correctly identifying which terms depend on $r$ and which do not.
  • Show algebraically that LGET recovers Newtonian gravity in the limit $M/M_0 \ll 1$, and state quantitatively why low-mass objects like the Sun have accumulated negligible gravitational legacy.
  • Explain why the exponential saturation factor $(1-e^{-M/M_0})$ is preferable to a power law for large-$M$ behaviour, and why the lasting effect must have a natural ceiling.
  • Write down the particle Lagrangian for LGET and apply the Euler-Lagrange equation to recover the force law.
  • Identify the three fields ($\phi$, $A_\mu$, $\chi$) in the relativistic action, state which lasting-effect component each controls, and explain the role of the coupling function $f(\phi)$.
  • State the modified Poisson equation and growth equation for cosmological perturbations, and explain how the accumulated lasting effect drives faster structure growth, compensating for the absence of dark matter in $P(k)$.
  • Implement the LGET force law in an N-body simulation kernel, identifying which factor encodes the fixed gravitational legacy of each source particle and can be pre-computed.
  • Compare LGET with Newtonian gravity, MOND, GR/$\Lambda$CDM, and $f(R)$: give one advantage and one limitation of each relative to LGET.
  • Describe the Bullet Cluster and explain why the spatial offset between lensing mass and baryonic gas is the single biggest observational challenge for LGET.
