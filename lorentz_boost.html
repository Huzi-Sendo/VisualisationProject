Why Galilean Relativity Fails

In classical mechanics, two frames S and S′ moving at relative velocity v are related by x′ = x − vt and t′ = t. The second equation encodes universal time. But if light travels at c in S, the Galilean rule gives c − v in S′, contradicting Einstein’s second postulate. The equation t′ = t must be replaced.

The Lorentz Transformation

The unique linear transformation preserving the speed of light between inertial frames is:

x′ = γ(x − vt)      t′ = γ(t − vx/c²)
γ1 / √(1 − β²) — Lorentz factor. Always ≥ 1. βv/c — velocity as a fraction of the speed of light. y′ = y, z′ = zTransverse dimensions unchanged.

The key difference from Galilean is the −vx/c² term: time in S′ depends on both time and position in S. This is the mathematical origin of the relativity of simultaneity.

Axis Tilting

A Lorentz boost tilts both the ct′ and x′ axes toward the 45° light cone line by equal angles θ = arctan(β). Both axes close in symmetrically. As β → 1 they collapse onto the light line — the geometric reason nothing with mass can reach c.

ct′ axis:   x = β · ct     (worldline of S′ origin in S)
x′ axis:    ct = β · x     (surface t′ = 0 in S)

Three Consequences

Time dilationΔt = γΔt′. Moving clock runs slow. ct′ tick marks are spaced γ apart in S coordinates. Length contractionL = L′/γ. Moving rods are shorter. Follows from simultaneous endpoint measurement in S. SimultaneityΔt′ = −γβΔx/c for events with Δt = 0. Simultaneous in S means time-separated in S′ when Δx ≠ 0.

The Invariant Hyperbola

The Minkowski interval ds² = (cΔt)² − Δx² is the same for all observers. Events at constant ds² = τ² from the origin trace a hyperbola: (ct)² − x² = τ². A boost slides events along the curve without leaving it — just as a rotation slides points along a circle. The ct′ tick marks always sit on these hyperbolas, confirming proper time τ is frame-independent.

ds² = (cΔt)² − Δx²   (invariant under all boosts)

Placed Events

Click anywhere on the diagram to place an event. Its S coordinates (ct, x) are read directly from the diagram. Its S′ coordinates (ct′, x′) are computed by the Lorentz transformation in real time. Drag β and watch the S′ coordinates change while the S coordinates stay fixed — this directly demonstrates that the same event has different coordinates in different frames.

Velocity Addition

u = (u′ + v) / (1 + u′v/c²)

No combination of sub-light velocities can reach c. If u′ = c, then u = c regardless of v: light is always c.

What to look for

  • Axis symmetry: ct′ and x′ tilt by exactly equal angles, reflecting the hyperbolic “rotation” that preserves the light cone.
  • Simultaneity planes: Drag β from 0. The horizontal planes tilt, showing events once simultaneous in S are time-separated in S′.
  • Tick spacing: ct′ tick marks spread apart as β grows. This is time dilation made geometric.
  • Place an event: Click to place an event at (ct=3, x=2). Watch its ct′ and x′ values change as you drag β — same event, different coordinates.
  • Near light speed (β=0.99): Both axes nearly on the light line, γ ≈ 7. A moving clock ticks 7 times slower.
